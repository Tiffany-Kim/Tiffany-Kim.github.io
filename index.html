Devotion (2022): My Honest Review

Despite the minimal attention Top Gun: Maverick received at The 2023 Oscars, no one can tell me it wasn’t the most memorable film of 2022. As we near the 1-year anniversary of the release of Top Gun: Maverick, I recently found myself rewatching clips and looking to relive the action-packed experience. While doing so, I was reminded of a similar film called “Devotion”, which premiered not long after the Top Gun sequel and also starred Glen Powell, the same actor who played “Hangman” in Top Gun: Maverick. Devotion portrays the story of Ensign Jesse L. Brown, the first African-American aviator for the US Navy, and his Navy Fighter Squadron as they fight in the Korean War. I recalled my friend saying it was “basically another version of Top Gun”, also that “it was really, really sad”. I decided I’d give it a shot! My one sentence review of Devotion: “It had so much potential.” Ultimately, it was a disappointing film. For me, the biggest difference between a good film and a bad film is the amount of thinking it induces. And not in the way you would think. When I’m watching a good movie, I’m rarely thinking. Instead, I am deeply immersed in the story. The dialogue, and the characters transport me into their world and away from mine. On the other hand, when I’m watching a bad movie, a constant train of thoughts is running through my mind, usually a mix of confusion or irritation. Why did they have to cut the scene like that? Why is the dialogue so flat? What is the point of this character even? These are some of the many questions I asked myself as two hours passed by. I don’t want to compare this movie to Top Gun: Maverick because it’s not worthy of being compared, but the story depicted in Devotion is probably closer to reality. The truth is, not everyone in war dies in a dramatic way. People just die. From wounds, from infection, from starvation, and so on. And sadly, there is no hero that shows up just in time to save you. Maybe because Devotion didn’t have these elements, it was a less exciting cinematic experience. However, no one can deny how sad or realistic of a wartime portrayal it was. If this was the director’s aim, I think he did a satisfactory job. But the scene where Mohring crashes the Corsair into the ocean and dies? No one’s even going to try to save him? Everyone just stood at the edge of the carrier and stared at the water, as if they dropped something and were waiting for it to emerge to the surface.
